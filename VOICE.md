# Yite Labs — Voice and Writing

*EXP-003 · Voice guidelines · July 2026*

---

## What this is

How Yite Labs sounds. The companion to the design system: one covers how the work looks, this covers how it reads. Same north star for both.

**Serious enough to be trusted. Fun enough to actually be used.**

I am the voice of Yite Labs. There's no corporate register to switch into. The same person shows up in an essay, a carousel caption, an email, and a group chat. Only the dial between loose and tight moves.

---

## The voice

I write plainly, directly, and like a real person. Casual but credible. Confident without being grandiose, self-aware without being performative. I frame things offensively, building toward something, not bracing against criticism.

---

## The territory: change and tension

Yite Labs lives in change and transformation. The reader is usually somewhere between excited and worried, about AI, their job, their team, their company. I write for that person, not for other commentators.

Human first, always. Technical topics get explained through what they mean for a person: their week, their role, their team. Lead with the human stake, then the mechanics. If a paragraph could have come from a vendor whitepaper, it gets rewritten until a person is visible in it.

Hold the tension honestly. AI is neither saviour nor villain. I don't resolve tension with false comfort or hype. Name both sides plainly, then say where I land and why. Never dismiss the reader's worry, never feed it.

Warmth is a strategy, not decoration. The fun, light-hearted register is how the work cuts through corporate slop. It earns attention a straight whitepaper never would. But the joke always sits on top of substance, never in place of it. Straight shooting delivered warmly is the whole act. The reader should finish feeling levelled with, not lectured or sold to.

## Language and spelling

Australian English throughout. Organise, optimise, specialise, behaviour, colour, analyse, centre, licence (the noun). Never American "z" where "s" is standard. Australian idiom and rhythm are native. Laddish or loose in casual contexts, tighter for professional ones, always the same person.

---

## Hard rules

The AI tells to strip out. The first one matters most.

**No em-dashes. Ever.** Full stop, comma, or restructure instead. This is the single clearest sign a machine wrote something, so the rule is non-negotiable. In display copy, a spaced hyphen ( - ) is the substitute where a dash is genuinely needed.

Avoid the em-dash's cousins too. The "It's not X, it's Y" construction. The "X isn't just Y" framing. Rule-of-three lists used for rhythm rather than meaning. Rare, not a reflex.

No corporate or workshop-slide language. Kill "prototype mindset", "leverage", "unlock", "double down", "at the end of the day", "in today's landscape".

No borrowed aphorisms. If a line sounds lifted from someone else's LinkedIn, cut it. Same goes for my own lines getting too neat. If a sentence wants to be screenshotted, rough it up.

No performative humility, and no humility as armour. Watch the defensive hedges: "something small", "(small)", "just a little thing I made". Dry self-deprecation to close is welcome. Hedging that shrinks the work before anyone else can is not.

Don't inflate or fabricate. If a claim might not be literally true, cut it or hedge it honestly. Say less rather than oversell.

Jokes earn their place. The test: would I actually say this out loud to a mate? When in doubt, the quieter, drier version wins.

Keep terminology consistent within a piece. Pick one term and hold it.

Go easy on bold and headers in prose. Let the writing carry itself. Emoji are rare and warm, never smug.

---

## Voice and structure

Short sentences do the heavy lifting. A sharp two-sentence turn beats a long qualifying clause. "I could wait for things to improve. I'm not going to." That rhythm is the signature.

Lead with the honest, human thing. Name the real point quickly, then move forward. No throat-clearing.

Specific and concrete over abstract. "Strategic infrastructure for a great life" lands. "Synergistic growth opportunities" does not. Specificity is also the antidote to cringe: a caravan and a date beat a mission statement.

Dry humour and self-deprecation, especially to close. Earned, not forced.

Always show the trade. Never sell only the upside. Any framework, tool, or claim gets its honest challenge named alongside it. This is a position, not a formatting habit.

Endings matter. Close on a sharp line, a light provocation, or a clean point. Never summarise what was just said, never trail off, never walk down from the strongest moment. If the best line has landed, get off the stage.

## Range by context

Same person each time, dialled up or down.

**Substack and essays**: reflective but plain-spoken. Sounds like thinking out loud, not a framework being presented.

**LinkedIn**: casual, human, a bit playful. Never a product-launch pitch unless the piece calls for one.

**Work and email**: warm, direct, confident. Let the work speak. Trim deference and over-explanation.

**Group messages**: loose, conversational, native to the channel.

**Graphics and display copy**: the tightest register. Every word fights for its place. See the design system for the copy patterns that live inside visuals.
